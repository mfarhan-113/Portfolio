Brain Tumor Detection with CNN

Admin

Deep Learning, Medical Imaging

4-Nov-2024

7

Project Overview

I developed a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to detect brain tumors from MRI scans. The model classifies images into tumor vs. no-tumor (binary) and can be extended to multi-class (e.g., meningioma, glioma, pituitary) depending on the dataset. The goal is to assist clinical triage by providing fast, consistent screening support.

Dataset & Preprocessing
  • Standard MRI datasets; images resized to a fixed input resolution (e.g., 224×224).
  • Contrast normalization and skull‑stripping where applicable.
  • Data augmentation: flips, slight rotations, zoom, and intensity jitter to improve generalization.
  • Train/validation/test split with class balancing to address skew.
CNN Architecture
  • Sequential Conv2D blocks with BatchNorm and ReLU, followed by MaxPooling.
  • Dropout between dense layers to reduce overfitting.
  • Final dense layer with sigmoid (binary) or softmax (multi‑class) activation.
Training Strategy
  • Optimizer: Adam; loss: Binary or Categorical Cross‑Entropy; metrics: accuracy, precision, recall, F1.
  • Callbacks: early stopping and model checkpointing on validation performance.
  • Learning‑rate scheduling to stabilize convergence.
Results
  • High validation accuracy with strong sensitivity (recall) on tumor classes.
  • Confusion matrix review to reduce false negatives (critical in medical settings).
  • Robust real‑time inference on CPU/GPU for batch or single‑image predictions.
Deployment & Usage
  • Batch mode for analyzing folders of MRI scans.
  • Simple UI option to upload an image and receive prediction with confidence score.
  • Extensible to DICOM pipelines and PACS integration.
Future Work
  • Upgrade to transfer learning (e.g., ResNet, EfficientNet) for improved accuracy.
  • 3D CNNs on volumetric MRI sequences for richer spatial context.
  • Explainability (Grad‑CAM) to highlight salient tumor regions.
Ethical Note

This tool is intended for research and decision support only, not as a standalone diagnostic. Clinical decisions must remain with qualified medical professionals.
